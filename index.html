Analysis

Key findings

Four insights drawn from activity patterns, calorie burn, sedentary behavior, and device engagement data.

Finding 1 — Calorie Gap

Active users burn ~1,000 more calories per day

Very Active and Fairly Active users burn approximately 1,000 more calories daily than Sedentary and Lightly Active users. This significant gap shows that even moderate increases in daily movement can have a meaningful impact on energy expenditure — making it a powerful message for motivating low-activity users.

Finding 2 — Sedentary Pattern

Fairly Active users sit less than Very Active users

Counterintuitively, Fairly Active users log fewer sedentary hours than Very Active users. This suggests that consistent, moderate movement distributed throughout the day reduces overall sitting time more effectively than intense but concentrated workouts — a key insight for designing habit-nudge features.

Finding 3 — Timing

Heart rate peaks at noon; calories peak at 5–7 PM

Heart rate reaches its daily high around 12–16h, while calorie burn peaks later at 17–19h. This delayed metabolic response suggests that encouraging midday activity can maximize calorie burn in the hours that follow — directly applicable to notification timing strategy.

Finding 4 — Engagement

85% of users wear their device consistently

The vast majority of users are heavy device wearers, meaning they already have a strong daily habit of wearing the tracker. The challenge is not adoption — it is converting passive wearing into active behavior change.

Act

Marketing recommendations

Three data-driven recommendations for Bellabeat's Leaf tracker, focused on converting passive wearers into active users.

01
Audience — Low-Activity Users

Use the 1,000 kcal gap as a motivational message

The data shows a ~1,000 kcal daily difference between active and sedentary users. Rather than framing this as a gap to shame users, Bellabeat can flip it into an opportunity: show low-activity users how close they are to burning significantly more calories with small, incremental changes. In-app progress visualizations and milestone nudges can make this gap feel bridgeable, not overwhelming. Suggested message: "Just 30 more active minutes today could change everything."

02
Timing — Notifications & Content

Send movement reminders at midday, not just morning

Heart rate peaks around noon while calorie burn peaks in the late afternoon — suggesting the body is metabolically primed for activity at midday. Bellabeat should time in-app reminders and social content to reach users at lunch hour (12–14h), when a short burst of movement can trigger elevated calorie burn throughout the rest of the day.

03
Product — Feature Direction

Promote "movement snacking" for sedentary users

Fairly Active users log fewer sedentary hours than Very Active users — suggesting that short, frequent activity breaks are more effective at reducing sitting time than concentrated workouts. Bellabeat's Leaf tracker is ideal for this pattern: worn passively, it can vibrate to prompt movement breaks. Marketing should position the Leaf not as a "workout tracker" but as an "all-day movement companion" — accessible to users who don't identify as athletes.

Prepare

Data sources & limitations

Full transparency about what the data covers — and where it falls short.

Primary source

FitBit Fitness Tracker Data

Kaggle dataset, CC0 Public Domain license. Contains minute-level physical activity, heart rate, sleep logs, and daily step counts from 33 consenting FitBit users over April 12 – May 12, 2016.

Variables analyzed
  • Daily total steps & active minutes
  • Sedentary minutes per day
  • Hourly calorie burn
  • Hourly average heart rate
  • Device wear time (engagement level)
Known limitations
  • Small sample (n = 33) — limited statistical power
  • Data from 2016 — behavioral patterns may have shifted
  • No gender, age, or geographic metadata
  • Potential self-selection bias among participants
Cleaning steps (R)
  • Removed duplicate rows across all tables
  • Standardized date and time formats
  • Classified users by average daily steps into 4 activity types
  • Merged daily activity with hourly calorie and heart rate tables
